Reconstructing After Deconstruction: Building Forward with Intention

September 9, 2025

Learn how the two stages of reconstruction—integration and internalization—work, and how to use the RISE framework to turn periods of change into lasting, positive transformation.

In our last newsletter, we focused on disruption and deconstruction. In this edition, we shift our attention to reconstruction, a critical phase in transformation. The following quote perfectly captures its significance in intentional transformation:

"The secret of change is to focus all of your energy, not on fighting the old, but on building the new." — Socrates (character in Way of the Peaceful Warrior by Dan Millman)

Defining Reconstruction

If deconstruction is the necessary breakdown of old patterns, reconstruction is the purposeful building of new, more effective ones. It’s the phase where temporary adjustments evolve into lasting adaptations, and new ideas become ingrained in our daily lives. Reconstruction establishes new ideas, beliefs, and practices by intentionally integrating them into our routines.

Consider a procrastinator trying to acquire better work habits. By breaking projects into smaller daily tasks, they dismantle old behavior patterns and establish new, effective practices. As this new routine is repeated, it replaces last-minute rushes with a more predictable, controlled, and less stressful approach. In the end, workflows are transformed, productivity is enhanced, and stress is significantly reduced.

Reconstruction is complete when new ways of thinking, feeling, and acting are fully integrated and internalized. For a former procrastinator, planning and setting SMART goals become a normal part of their routine. This transformation marks the end of reconstruction phase and a successful transition cycle.

The stages of reconstruction

Reconstruction occurs in two distinct stages: integration and internalization. The integration stage entails the consious process of developing new mindsets and incorporating new practices. On the other hand, internalization involves transforming new practices into habitual, sustainable behaviors.

Integration: Combining the Old and New

Integration is the mindful practice of weaving the valuable lessons from our past with the opportunities of our present experiences. It's not about starting over, but about consciously curating our experiences. This means holding onto what serves us well while courageously letting go of what holds us back.

For example, someone feeling burned out from constantly saying "yes" can shift from people-pleasing to balanced helping without sacrificing their caring nature. By responding, "I can't take on the entire project, but I’m happy to review your first draft this afternoon," they honor both their values and their well-being. This approach preserves kindness while integrating self-respect and boundaries. More importantly, it combines old strengths with new practices, effectively.

By skillfully merging the old with the new, we create a clear and cohesive narrative that makes adaptation feel natural and meaningful. This narrative serves as a compass guiding us toward sustainable growth while protecting against unhealthy adaptations. Whether you're navigating a personal change, career transition, or leading organizational change, integration is an essential first step toward lasting and meaningful change.

Internalization: Embodying Healthy Habits that Stick

Internalization is the final step of transformation. New ideas, values, or habits become part of who you are and not just what you do. This shift marks the completion of reconstruction, where change is no longer effortful. When behaviors are fully integrated and feel natural, the reconstruction and transition process is complete.

For example, we can reconstruct a negative self-concept by deliberately reframing our inner dialogue. Our behavior is profoundly influenced by how we speak to ourselves. Rather than reacting to mistakes with shame and self-criticism, we can learn to respond with compassion. Reminding ourselves that mistakes are an essential part of the learning. Though this new narrative may feel unnatural at first, consistent practice helps internalize it. The result? Greater self-compassion, higher self-esteem, and a healthier self-concept.

Internalization is the stage where new practices and mindsets are woven into the fabric of our identity. What once required conscious effort now flows naturally. What once felt unfamiliar evolves into clarity and confidence. This stage marks the completion of the reconstruction phase and the beginning of lasting, self-sustaining change.

The RISE Framework: Reconstructing for Transformation

While deconstruction is about subtracting, reconstruction is about intentionally adding new layers of strength, wisdom, and capability to create something better than before. Transformation is not about erasing the past, but honoring its foundation. It means acknowledging what shaped us while consciously building something new that serves us well.

The RISE framework can serve as a useful tool to guide us through the process of reconstructing for transformation.

R – Recognize Your Foundation: Identify your core values, key strengths, and support system. Guiding Question: What values, skills, or relationships form my unshakable foundation?

Example: “I value kindness. My support system includes my close friend Sarah and my therapist.”

I – Imagine with Intention: Clarify your desired outcome. Visualize what meaningful change looks like. Guiding Question: What do I want to happen, and why does it matter?

Example: “I want to help others without burning out or sacrificing my kindness.”

S – Start and Support: Take one small step. Weave a new habit into your core values. Lean on your support network. Guiding Question: What’s one step I can take today?

Example: “Instead of saying ‘yes’ to everything, I’ll offer limited help. I’ll ask Sarah to check in with me weekly and discuss progress with my therapist.”

E – Embody and Evolve: Check in regularly. Adapt what is working. Practice until it becomes natural. Guiding Question: How will I know this is working? What needs to change?

Example: “Each week, I’ll reflect: Do I feel less drained? Is this feeling more natural?”

Reflection
